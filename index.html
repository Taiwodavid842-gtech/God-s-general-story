God's General Story
John Wesley
The Man God Used to Spark a Spiritual Revival
John Wesley was one of the greatest evangelists in Christian history. Through his preaching, teaching, and disciplined Christian life, God used him to ignite a spiritual revival in Britain during the eighteenth century. His ministry led to the rise of the Methodist movement and influenced millions of believers around the world. Early Life John Wesley was born on June 17, 1703, in Epworth. He was the fifteenth of nineteen children born to Samuel Wesley, an Anglican minister, and Susanna Wesley, a deeply devoted Christian who personally taught and discipled her children. When John was only five years old, their family home caught fire. He was rescued just moments before the roof collapsed. This experience deeply shaped his life, and he later described himself as "a brand plucked from the burning," believing God had spared him for a special purpose. Education John was an excellent student. He attended Christ Church, Oxford, where he excelled in theology, classics, and philosophy. After graduating, he became a fellow of Lincoln College and was ordained as a minister in the Church of England. The Holy Club While at Oxford, John, his brother Charles Wesley, and a small group of friends met regularly for Bible study, prayer, fasting, and helping the poor. Because of their strict discipline, other students mockingly called them "Methodists." Instead of being offended, the name eventually became associated with a worldwide Christian movement. Mission to America In 1735, John traveled to the American colony of Georgia as a missionary. He hoped to convert others but later admitted that he himself lacked the assurance of salvation. The mission was difficult and ended in disappointment. He returned to England discouraged, asking himself: "I went to America to convert the Indians, but who shall convert me?" His Conversion Everything changed on May 24, 1738. While attending a meeting on Aldersgate Street, Wesley listened to a reading from Martin Luther's introduction to the Book of Romans. He later wrote in his journal: "I felt my heart strangely warmed. I felt I did trust in Christ, Christ alone for salvation." This became the turning point of his ministry. A Life of Revival After his conversion, Wesley began preaching throughout Britain. When many churches refused to invite him, he accepted the encouragement of George Whitefield and began preaching outdoors. He preached in fields, marketplaces, streets, mines, and wherever people would gather. Thousands came to hear the Gospel. Tireless Ministry John Wesley traveled an estimated 250,000 miles, mostly on horseback. He preached more than 40,000 sermons during his lifetime. He often preached several times in a single day and rose before dawn to pray and study the Bible. Despite harsh weather, poor roads, and advancing age, he remained faithful to his calling. Challenges John Wesley faced many hardships. 1. Rejection Many churches refused to allow him to preach. 2. Persecution Crowds sometimes threw stones, attacked him, and disrupted his meetings. 3. Long Travels Traveling by horseback exposed him to accidents, harsh weather, and physical exhaustion. 4. Marriage Difficulties His marriage to Mary Vazeille Wesley was unhappy. His constant travels and ministry commitments created tension, and they eventually separated. 5. Criticism Some criticized his emphasis on disciplined Christian living and organized societies. Yet Wesley continued preaching Christ with humility and perseverance. His Successes God used John Wesley in extraordinary ways. He: Sparked the Evangelical Revival in Britain. Founded the Methodist movement. Organized Bible study groups and discipleship classes. Encouraged evangelism among ordinary believers. Helped establish schools and charitable works. Inspired missionaries to carry the Gospel around the world. His brother Charles wrote over 6,000 hymns, many of which strengthened the movement. His Teachings John Wesley emphasized: Salvation by God's grace through faith. Personal holiness. Daily prayer and Bible study. Love for God and neighbor. Evangelism. Practical Christianity expressed through acts of mercy. Spiritual discipline. One of his best-known sayings is: "Do all the good you can, by all the means you can, in all the ways you can, in all the places you can, at all the times you can, to all the people you can, as long as ever you can." His Final Years Even in his eighties, Wesley continued traveling and preaching. On March 2, 1791, at the age of 87, he peacefully went home to be with the Lord. His reported final words were: "The best of all is, God is with us." Lessons from John Wesley's Life 1. Holiness Matters A transformed heart should produce a transformed life. 2. Never Stop Sharing the Gospel The Gospel is for everyone, not only those inside church buildings. 3. Discipline Builds Spiritual Strength Prayer, Bible study, fasting, and fellowship help believers grow. 4. Persevere Through Opposition God's calling is worth following even when others oppose you. 5. Leave a Lasting Legacy John Wesley's ministry continues to influence churches and Christians more than two centuries after his death. Conclusion John Wesley's life demonstrates what God can do through one person who is fully committed to Christ. Though he faced rejection, criticism, physical hardship, and personal disappointment, he remained faithful to preaching the Gospel and calling believers to holy living. His legacy reminds us that a life of prayer, discipline, love, and obedience to God can transform not only individuals but entire nations.
