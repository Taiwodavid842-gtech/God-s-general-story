God's General Story
William Tyndale
The Man Who Gave the English People the Bible
William Tyndale was one of the greatest Christian reformers in history. He devoted his life to translating the Bible into English so that ordinary people could read God's Word for themselves. His courage, scholarship, and willingness to suffer for the truth made him one of the most influential figures of the Protestant Reformation. Early Life William Tyndale was born around 1494 in Gloucestershire. Little is known about his childhood, but he showed exceptional intelligence from an early age. He grew up during a time when the Bible was mainly available in Latin, a language most ordinary people could not understand. Many church leaders discouraged the translation of the Bible into English because they feared it would lead to misunderstanding and challenges to church authority. Education Tyndale studied at Magdalen Hall, University of Oxford, where he earned degrees in the liberal arts and theology. Later, he also studied at University of Cambridge. He became fluent in several languages, including: English Latin Greek Hebrew German French Italian Spanish His knowledge of Greek and Hebrew allowed him to translate the Bible directly from the original biblical languages rather than relying only on the Latin translation. His Burden for the Bible While serving as a tutor in a wealthy English household, Tyndale often discussed the Bible with local religious leaders. During one debate, a church official argued that people needed the church's traditions more than God's Word. Tyndale responded with words that became famous: "If God spare my life, before many years I will cause a boy that driveth the plough to know more of the Scripture than thou dost." This became his life's mission. Beginning the Translation Tyndale first sought permission to translate the Bible into English. His request was refused. Realizing that he could not complete the work safely in England, he left the country in 1524. He never returned. Living as a Fugitive Tyndale moved between cities in what is now Germany and the Low Countries, living under constant threat. He often had to: Change locations. Use false names. Work in secret. Depend on friends for protection. Despite these dangers, he continued translating the Scriptures. Printing the English New Testament In 1526, Tyndale completed and printed the first English New Testament translated directly from the Greek. Thousands of copies were secretly smuggled into England. Many people eagerly bought and read the Scriptures in their own language for the first time. Church authorities confiscated and burned many copies, but more continued to be printed. Ironically, money from purchased copies that were burned helped finance the printing of additional editions. Translating the Old Testament Tyndale did not stop with the New Testament. He translated: The Pentateuch (Genesis–Deuteronomy). Jonah. Other portions of the Old Testament. His goal was to complete the entire Bible in English. Although he was arrested before finishing the whole project, his work became the foundation for later English translations. Scholars estimate that much of the language in the later King James Version was influenced by Tyndale's wording. Challenges William Tyndale's life was filled with hardship. 1. Constant Danger He spent years hiding from those who wanted him arrested. 2. Loneliness Living in exile meant separation from his homeland, friends, and family. 3. Poverty Much of his work was done with limited resources. 4. Opposition Many powerful religious and political leaders opposed his translation efforts. 5. Betrayal In 1535, a man named Henry Phillips gained Tyndale's trust and betrayed him to the authorities. Imprisonment After his arrest, Tyndale was imprisoned for about 16 months in the castle at Vilvoorde. Even in prison, he remained devoted to studying Scripture. In a famous letter, he asked for: A warmer coat. A hat. Candles. His Hebrew Bible and grammar books so he could continue studying. This request reveals his deep love for God's Word, even while awaiting trial. Martyrdom In 1536, William Tyndale was tried for heresy. He was condemned to death. On October 6, 1536, he was first strangled and then his body was burned at the stake. His reported final prayer was: "Lord, open the King of England's eyes." Answer to His Prayer Only a few years after Tyndale's death, King Henry VIII authorized an English Bible for use in churches. Later English translations, including the Great Bible, the Geneva Bible, and especially the King James Version, were deeply influenced by Tyndale's work. His dream—that ordinary people could read God's Word in English—became a reality. His Successes Although he died as a martyr, Tyndale achieved lasting success. He: Produced the first printed English New Testament translated directly from Greek. Began translating the Old Testament from Hebrew. Influenced nearly every major English Bible translation that followed. Helped make the Bible accessible to ordinary people. Advanced the Protestant Reformation through his commitment to Scripture. Lessons from William Tyndale's Life 1. Love God's Word The Bible is worth studying, sharing, and preserving. 2. Obey God Above Human Approval Tyndale remained faithful despite intense opposition. 3. Persevere Through Hardship He continued his work in exile, poverty, and prison. 4. Leave a Legacy His work has blessed generations of English-speaking Christians. 5. The Truth Cannot Be Silenced Though his enemies tried to destroy his work, God's Word continued to spread. Conclusion William Tyndale's life is one of extraordinary courage, sacrifice, and devotion to God's Word. He gave up comfort, safety, and ultimately his life so that ordinary men and women could read the Bible in their own language. His legacy lives on in the English Bible and in the millions of believers who have encountered God's truth through translations influenced by his work. His story reminds us that one person, wholly committed to God's Word, can change the course of history.
